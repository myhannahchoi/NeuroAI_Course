Tutorial Objectives#

Estimated timing of tutorial: 35 minutes

In this tutorial, we will explore the challenges when the distributions of two datasets stray from each other and thus observe how performance degrades as testing data distribution strays from training distribution.

Those can be usually categorized into covariate or concept shifts. The first one aims to generalize the situations where distribution of features was changed. For example, consider the dog/cat classification task where the model was trained to differentiate between real-photos of pets while the testing dataset consists fully of cartoon characters. The latter, as it name suggests, is more of the conceptual change in the relationship between features and desired predicted output. For example, the recommendation system adapts to the userâ€™s preferences, thus changing its predictions to suit the needs.

We will explore both of the shifts on the simple function which proposes the relationship between day in the year and price for the fruits in the local market.

Coding Exercise 1: Fitting pricing data to MLP#

In this exercise we are going to train Multi-Layer Perceptron (fully-connected neural network with non-linear ReLU activation functions) to make it predicting prices for fruits. Still, we are going to supply information only about summer months and in the next interactive demo, we will explore how well it generalizes to other months.

As it was mentioned in the video, we will model the price of the fruits with the corresponding function:

\[f(x) = A x^{2} + B sin(\pi x + \phi) + C\]

meaning that we have quadratic annual behaviour (with summer months being at the bottom of the parabola) as well as bi-weekly seasonality introduced by \(sin(\pi x)\) term (with top values being the days where there is supply of fresh fruits to the market). Variables \(A, B, \phi \:\: \text{and} \:\: C\) allow us to tune the day-price relation in different cases (for example, we will observe the role of \(\phi\) in the second section of the tutorial). For this particular one, let us take \(A = 0.005\), \(B = 0.1\), \(\phi = 0\) and \(C = 1\).

At first, letâ€™s take a look at the data - we will plot it.

Now, we are ready to train the model to predict the price of the fruits. For that, we need to take data only about summer months and feed it into MLP. As usually, the data is going to be separated into 2 distinct classes: train & test - so that we could measure its performormance with R-squared metric. We will also normalize the data to provide better learning stability for the model.

The MLP consists of 2 hidden layers with 100 neurons in each. We use LBFGS as solver for this partiuclar scenario as it performs better, compared to Adam or SGD, when the number of data points is limited.

Coding Exercise 2: Change in day of supply#

As discussed in the video, the concept shift in the context of fruits prices relation, might be introduced via the change in the day of the week when fresh fruits supply takes place. Let us take a look at the modeling equation again:

\[f(x) = A x^{2} + B sin(\pi x + \phi) + C\]

Which variable, do you think, we need to change, so that now we would receive fresh fruits, letâ€™s say, 2 days later than it was before?

Answer
Yep, indeed, it is all about sinusoid phase shift - we only need to change $\phi$ value.

Let us take a look how well model generalizes to this unexpected change as well.

shifted_phi = - 2 * np.pi / 7
shifted_prices = A * days**2 + B * np.sin(np.pi*days + shifted_phi) + C

#plot relation between days, prices & shifted prices for June
with plt.xkcd():
  plt.plot(days[151:181], prices[151:181], label = "Original Data")
  plt.plot(days[151:181], shifted_prices[151:181], label = "Shifted Data")
  plt.xlabel('Day')
  plt.ylabel('Price')
  plt.legend()
  plt.show()
